## 摘要

<p>This study numerically investigates the large-strain shearing behaviour of rigid–soft granular mixtures, with particular emphasis on the combined effects of rigid-grain shape and soft-grain fraction. A coupled mesh-free continuum–discrete framework is employed to perform simple shear tests on mixtures composed of polygonal rigid grains, ranging from angular pentagons to rounded discs, and deformable inclusions with varying content. Macroscopically, the critical-state stress ratio decreases with increasing soft-grain fraction for angular-grain packings, whereas this effect progressively weakens with decreasing grain angularity, and vanishes for disc-based assemblies. Micromechanical analysis indicates that this behaviour is governed by the competition between the opposite dependencies of contact orientation anisotropy and tangential force anisotropy on soft-grain fraction. Furthermore, a mesoscale cell-based tessellation is adopted to indicate that, in all systems, the evolution of the mean cell elongation and mean cell stress ratio mirrors the corresponding macroscopic stress response. This approach reveals the universal features of cell statistics in rigid–soft granular systems, with cell order distributions following a theoretically derived exponential decay, and normalised cell stress ratio distributions collapsing onto a single Weibull distribution. These insights establish a direct link between micro- and mesoscale self-organisation and macroscopic material strength for understanding and modelling rigid–soft granular mixtures.</p>
