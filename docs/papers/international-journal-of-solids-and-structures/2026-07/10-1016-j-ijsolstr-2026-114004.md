# A unified variational differential quadrature method-based framework for elastic wave attenuation in two-dimensional metamaterials

## 摘要

<p>This paper introduces a unified semi-analytical framework for wave propagation in both infinite periodic lattices and finite arrays of two-dimensional (2D) metamaterials. The formulation is constructed within a weak-form setting, and applies the variational differential quadrature method (VDQM) on Chebyshev–Gauss–Lobatto (CGL) grids to obtain compact matrix operators without explicit high-order strong-form differentiation. Ligament-level stiffness and mass matrices are then assembled using finite-element-style connectivity through shared degrees of freedom (DOFs), enabling systematic modeling of multi-member unit cells. For the infinite metamaterial, periodic boundary conditions (PBCs) are enforced algebraically by projecting the unit cell DOFs onto the null space of the constraint system, yielding a reduced generalized eigenvalue problem for dispersion calculations. The same assembled model is also used to compute the transmission response of finite arrays, allowing attenuation peaks, band gap persistence, and period-dependent scaling to be quantified directly. The proposed framework is initially validated against the conventional re-entrant honeycomb (RH) as an established literature benchmark. Subsequently, it is utilized for an extensive analysis of a novel 2D re-entrant and tooth-shaped hybrid metamaterial (RTHM), formed by augmenting a baseline RH with tooth-shaped elements. To ensure rigorous predictive accuracy, the finite-array transmission responses of both the RH and RTHM are cross-verified between an in-house MATLAB implementation and COMSOL Multiphysics. Consequently, convergence and validation studies demonstrate numerical stability and rapid convergence, yielding results that are highly consistent across dispersion curves, iso-frequency contours, and low-frequency attenuation measures. The proposed RTHM delivers excellent low-frequency attenuation through a combination of robust band gap formation and strong waveguiding effects, as evidenced by the transmission response together with group velocity maps and directivity plots. Collectively, this study contributes both a new unified framework for wave propagation in infinite and finite periodic systems and a highly effective model for low-frequency wave attenuation, enabling the systematic design and optimization of next-generation metamaterials.</p>
