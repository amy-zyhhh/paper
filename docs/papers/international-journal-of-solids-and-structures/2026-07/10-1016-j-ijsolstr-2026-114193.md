# Interplay of vacuum pressure and adhesion in the pull-off force of octopus-inspired adhesives

## 摘要

<p>The ability to attach to and release from objects is critical for both engineered and biological systems in tasks such as manipulation, assembly, and locomotion. Key mechanisms which govern attachment are interfacial adhesion and suction. Adhesion originates from molecular attraction across contacting surfaces, while suction arises from a vacuum pressure (i.e., a pressure differential relative to the ambient) generated within a sealed cavity between those surfaces. The force required for detachment depends on both the magnitudes of vacuum pressure and interfacial adhesion, as well as the area over which the vacuum pressure acts, making it difficult to be predicted. Here, we study the mechanics of a trumpet-shaped, octopus-inspired adhesive device, in which the cavity for generating vacuum pressure is sealed by an adhesive interface. We use finite element modeling to simulate the cavity deformation and the evolution of the adhesive interface during detachment. The numerical results, in good agreement with experiments, reveal that deformation caused by vacuum pressure can affect the contribution of interfacial adhesion in resisting detachment, whereas variations in adhesive properties can modulate the vacuum pressure and its acting area. These observations demonstrate that vacuum pressure and interfacial adhesion are inherently coupled rather than independent. This finding provides insights into the interplay between suction and adhesion in the performance of attachment devices.</p>
