## 摘要

<p>Transfer integration of planar devices onto doubly curved surfaces is fundamentally constrained by the non-isometric nature of plane-to-surface attachment. This capability is central to emerging three-dimensional (3D) conformal electronic systems that must operate on curved biological tissues, robotic skins, and optical surfaces. Wrap-like transfer printing provides an effective strategy to achieve such 3D integration, but its underlying mechanics remain insufficiently understood. The geometric incompatibility between a planar precursor and a doubly curved target not only distorts the transported pattern but also stores elastic energy that may destabilize the post-wrapping state. Here, we formulate petal-to-sphere wrapping as a finite deformation problem and establish a predictive framework that unifies attachment, pattern transport, and interfacial stability. Starting from a finite deformation Kirchhoff-Love plate model, we derive a mechanics-consistent attachment map for the fully wrapped configuration. This map removes geometric ambiguity and enables deterministic sphere-to-plane and plane-to-sphere pattern transport, providing a direct route for inverse pre-distortion design of planar layouts for prescribed spherical targets. We further formulate an energetic criterion for post-wrapping edge delamination and obtain the corresponding stability boundary and phase diagram in terms of petal geometry, sheet stiffness, and interfacial toughness. A scaling analysis further uncovers a transition from a bending-influenced regime at small petal width to a stretching-dominated regime at large petal width, and identifies distinct scaling laws in the two limits. The theoretical predictions are validated by finite element simulations. These results establish a predictive mechanics foundation for both geometric design and post-wrapping reliability control in wrap-like transfer printing.</p>
