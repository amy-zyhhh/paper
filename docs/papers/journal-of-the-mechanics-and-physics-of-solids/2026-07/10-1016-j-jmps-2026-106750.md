## 摘要

<p>Accurate prediction of time-dependent contact area evolution is critical to the lifetime and reliability of interfacial adhesive systems. Although contact creep and relaxation under compressive loading have been extensively studied, the evolution of interfacial contact area under tensile loading remains insufficiently understood, particularly over long timescales. Recent work has identified distinct adhesion scenarios for a rigid sphere adhered to a viscoelastic substrate under constant tensile loading, including detachment and persistent adherence, and has revealed a counterintuitive phenomenon of contact growth. However, the underlying driving mechanism, the criterion governing its occurrence, and its limiting extent have remained unresolved. Here, we investigate the problem by examining the competition between the adhesive traction force F at at the contact edge and the applied tensile load P. Building on this perspective, we extend the classic Maugis–Dugdale (M–D) model by incorporating bulk viscoelasticity and interfacial dissipation to describe long-term interfacial evolution under constant tensile loading. The proposed model elucidates the physical origin of contact growth and establishes the criterion for its onset as F at &gt; P. Furthermore, the saturation limit of growth is determined and shown to be governed jointly by a creep limit associated with full material relaxation and a traction limit imposed by interfacial strength. The model is validated against experimental results, and the effects of geometry, applied load, preloading, and viscoelastic parameters on interfacial evolution are systematically examined, leading to adhesion maps for predicting stable contact and contact growth. These findings provide a mechanistic basis for understanding long-term adhesion and a predictive framework for controlling interfacial behavior under complex service conditions.</p>
