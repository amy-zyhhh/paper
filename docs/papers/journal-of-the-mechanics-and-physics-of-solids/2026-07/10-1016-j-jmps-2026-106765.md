# Generalizing Resistive Force Theory: Revealing and Modeling Nonlinear Intrusion Resistance in Solid-like Granular Matter

## 摘要

<p>Nonlinear resistance during intrusion into solid-like granular matter remains an open problem, challenging the linear drag paradigm underlying widely used models such as Resistive Force Theory (RFT). Here we combine DEM simulations, experiments, and theoretical analysis to uncover the physical origin of this nonlinear response and establish a quantitative theory for its full evolution. We show that the resistance emerges from an intrusion-induced structural evolution of the granular matter, effectively described by the packing fraction, and saturates only when the system reaches a fully developed steady state. For this regime, granular characteristic paths theory yields a parameter-free expression for the steady-state resistance that depends solely on the internal friction angle of the granular matter. Integrating the progressive structural evolution with the steady-state resistance law, we obtain a predictive model that captures the complete nonlinear resistance process during horizontal intrusion. We further demonstrate that the RFT framework cannot capture the early nonlinear regime because granular compressibility induces radially distributed slip trajectories, thereby violating the local independence assumption on which RFT is built. On this basis, we propose a simple and general criterion for assessing the validity of RFT across different soft materials and intrusion conditions. Our results reveal that, unlike in continuous media, nonlinear resistance in granular matter is fundamentally governed by intrusion-induced structural evolution, providing a general physical framework for intrusion-driven processes in robotic terradynamics, geomechanics, and planetary exploration.</p>
