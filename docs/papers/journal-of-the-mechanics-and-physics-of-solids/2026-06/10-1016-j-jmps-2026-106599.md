# A unified transient network theory for viscoplastic response of dynamic polymer networks

## 摘要

<p>The Transient Network Theory (TNT) offers a physically grounded continuum framework for modeling the mechanics of dynamic polymer networks, where the bonds continuously dissociate (or break) and (re)associate (or reform) over time. The strength of the TNT lies in its bottom-up molecular approach, which links macroscopic mechanical response to microscopic chain-level kinetics, thereby predicting viscoplastic flow as an emergent outcome of reversible bond dynamics. Since its inception by Green and Tobolsky (1946), TNT has been extensively applied to study polymer rheology; however, it has never been examined within the broader context of alternative, more traditional theories, such as finite viscoplasticity. The latter adopts a thermodynamically consistent, top-down approach typically based on (1) the multiplicative decomposition of the deformation gradient into elastic and inelastic parts, and (2) the principle of maximum plastic dissipation. This raises a fundamental question: in what ways is the viscoplastic behavior of dynamic polymer networks, as predicted by TNT, different from that of finite viscoplasticity? Understanding this distinction is essential for novel material design and synthesis, as it informs the selection of the most appropriate theoretical framework for accurately modeling and predicting material response. The objective of this study is twofold. First, to perform a detailed comparative analysis between TNT and finite viscoplasticity, demonstrating TNT’s suitability for modeling dynamic polymer networks. To this end, we conduct a rigorous mathematical examination of TNT within the setting of continuum mechanics, drawing explicit parallels with classical formulations, such as those by Simo (1998). By integrating polymer physics and continuum mechanics, we also reformulate the TNT in a unified manner. The resulting unified framework, validated against kinetic Monte Carlo simulations, unveils new mechanistic insights into the distinct roles of chain association and dissociation in governing macroscopic flow. Second, the unified TNT extends the conventional version to encompass both compressible and incompressible responses, thereby broadening its applicability to a diverse range of materials, including dynamic hydrogels. This involves presenting a systematic thermodynamically motivated approach to prescribe flow rules within TNT.</p>
