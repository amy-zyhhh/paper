## 摘要

<p>Transparent conductive films, owing to their combination of high electrical conductivity and high optical transmittance, are widely applied in modern electronic and optoelectronic devices. Nevertheless, their electrical, thermal, and mechanical stability issues in high-frequency electromagnetic environments are becoming increasingly prominent, with microwave damage, in particular, emerging as a critical bottleneck limiting their development. This study established an experimental system for simultaneous in-situ monitoring of temperature and damage under microwave irradiation, investigating the thermo-electro-mechanical damage evolution and failure mechanisms of indium tin oxide (ITO) transparent conductive film. Firstly, experimental findings revealed an edge effect of microwave hotspots. Simulations indicate that the underlying mechanism is the variation in thin film conductivity, which drives the transition from uniform heating dominated by dielectric loss to the formation of edge hotspots dominated by conduction loss. Secondly, microwave discharge was observed to generate at the mid-position of the film edge. This phenomenon originates from microcracks formed in the ITO layer due to tensile stress generated at edge hotspots, which alters the electromagnetic field distribution and leads to electric field concentration at the microcrack tips. Thirdly, the crack propagation lagging behind the discharge trajectory was captured. It was revealed that thermal stress concentration, induced by the localized high temperatures generated by microwave discharge, is the key mechanism driving the cracking of the substrate. In addition, a strategy was proposed whereby increasing the film thickness would prevent the occurrence of microcrack. These findings serve as direct evidence for a comprehensive understanding of microwave damage of conductive thin films.</p>
