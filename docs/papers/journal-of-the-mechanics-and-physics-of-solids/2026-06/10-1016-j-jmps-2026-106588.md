# Unraveling negative strain-rate sensitivity of a quenching &amp; partitioning steel by a crystal plasticity model coupling solute-drag effect

## 摘要

<p>Advanced high-strength steels (AHSS) are crucial for lightweight, crashworthy automotive components, where high-strain-rate deformation is a key design consideration. Unlike traditional AHSS, which typically exhibit positive strain-rate sensitivity, recently developed quenching and partitioning (Q&amp;P) steels display an anomalous negative strain-rate sensitivity. This phenomenon eludes conventional crystal plasticity models, which lack a physical description of solute-dislocation interactions. To address this limitation, we develop a crystal plasticity framework explicitly coupled to a novel solute-drag model that mechanistically links the spatiotemporal evolution of carbon atmospheres around dislocations to the dislocation waiting time via two diffusion mechanisms: cross-core diffusion and continuum diffusion. The framework models the multi-phase microstructure of Q&amp;P steels, incorporating dislocation slip and deformation-induced martensitic transformation in retained austenite, and dislocation slip with the solute-drag effect in martensite. With a single parameter set, the model accurately reproduces stress–strain responses under continuous loading across a wide range of strain rates and temperatures, and successfully predicts unfitted behavior under interrupted loading histories, including the evolution of dislocation density and phase fractions. It is found that the negative strain-rate sensitivity originates predominantly from a severe, localized reduction of solute drag within critically stressed slip systems at high strain rates. This study provides a robust, microstructure-aware modeling framework for solute-dislocation interactions with slip-system resolution, advances the fundamental understanding of carbon segregation effects in multi-phase steels, and offers guidance for designing next-generation structural materials.</p>
